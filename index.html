The Merrill Person

What makes a Merrillite?

In this project, we decided to focus on the concept of what it means to be in Merrill. As first years, we are welcomed into a community and put in a class without really knowing what we are getting ourselves into. The Merrill theme is Cultural Identities and Global Consciousness. We wanted to know how that theme is reflected into the values and teachings of Merrillites. We interviewed members of the Merrill staff and other Merrill students to answer our main question, “Who decides what Merrill stands for?” Through our research and interviews, we were able to explore and expand on our question and find more about what it means to be a Merrill affiliate.

Interview #1: Aims McGuinness, Merrill Provost

Sean: Are Merrill Affiliates chosen? If so, how?

Aims: The short answer is that when students are admitted here, and then they accept admission, then they have an opportunity to choose their college. But most students, interestingly, don't actually choose. They may not even be aware that they have a choice. You know, it's at admissions time; a lot is going on in people's lives. They don't necessarily see that email. And so most students at UC Santa Cruz end up being assigned by a process that is in part, I think, if it's not an algorithm, it's similar to an algorithm. And the algorithm, I think, really focuses on demography, demographic characteristics, so they want to create roughly comparable diversity in each college, as I just shared with you. Actually, it doesn't work out exactly that way since Merrill and Oakes are more diverse than the other colleges so there you are. So even though most Merrill affiliates are not here because they wanted to be here from the start, one thing I find, though, is that very quickly—certainly by the time people graduate—people do feel a sense of connection here at Merrill that is distinct and in some ways, arguably, I think, stronger than what you find in many other colleges.

When students ask me about the theme, they often are kind of skeptical of it. It sounds like a brand name, something that could be on a cereal box. But then, when I asked students, like last year, when they were graduating, is the theme meaningful to you? And they were saying, “Yeah, actually it is,” and it became meaningful through the core course, it became meaningful even more with the interactions with other Merrill students. And they really did, actually, they imbued it with meaning. And partly, it's the space of the college; I find it so beautiful. There's this enclosed space around the Merrill lawn, there are nice places to hang out in the sun, at Cowell, at Porter, and other colleges, but there's something special about this lawn, especially like right now. For instance, I am very sad and stressed and very exhausted because of recent events, and I love just being here on the lawn and being here with the Merrill Chihuahua, seeing students hang out [...] So I find this kind of like, it feels kind of sheltered here, and I'm ever surrounded by the redwoods. I think that's really, really beautiful.

And weirdly, some of it is partly our altitude. This is something that students complain about, you know, like “I have to, I have to go up that hill.” And I feel that when I'm late for a meeting and I'm like, going up that inaccessible staircase, which is too thin; I'm thinking like, “Man, I wish that I did. Wish I didn't have to come up this hill.” But an interesting other effect of this hill is that once you do get up here, it feels like I made it. It can feel kind of like an oasis to me and the lawn can feel that way. And I mean, many students tell me, you know, I've had experiences like with [my friends] in Porter or Cowell or Stevenson, I say, “Let's hang out at Merrill”, and they're like, “No, it's like, too hard to get up there”, and that can hurt people's feelings. On the other hand, maybe that's okay, you know, if some people don't want to make the effort to come here to be on the Merrill lawn. No judgment there. You know, they get to make their own choices. And it gives us, in an interesting way, a kind of sense of maybe some of the benefits of exclusiveness without actually excluding, because everybody is welcome to come up to the hill, right? But not everybody makes the effort. And so it does mean that, yeah. So I think that creates a sense of a kind of intimacy and community here that allows students, from what they tell me, they really treasure.

Eli: What amount of influence do you have on the Core Course?

Aims: As the provost, my title is the chief academic officer of the college; so ultimately, I am responsible for the content of the core course and for hiring the instructors and leading the faculty and shaping the course in different ways. Well, I just started as provost, so this is only my second year. What happened last year was basically [that] I was new here, and I thought, well, let's just go with what we've been doing. Many students find the course meaningful, and instructors are enjoying the course, and so let's just leave things as they are. And then, you know, as I spoke to students, however, over the course of the year, I came to learn that lessons wanted the core course to be different, or they weren't connecting to it. They weren't finding it meaningful. And so I spoke about this with Merrill Student Government, and I asked Merrill Student Government, “What do you think? What do you think the core course should be?”. So I started just going to the Merrill Student Government meetings and getting people's opinions. And then we did surveys, and students actually really used their voice and said, “We like these things, but we don't like these other things.” And then, based on those surveys we hired four students to redesign the course over the summer based on surveys, research that they did, and others. So the course is actually much different this year than it was last year, in part because of our efforts to listen to students, and we'll be doing that again this year. So here are some examples of things that changed because of student demands or student concerns:

  • Students last year and previous years liked the nature hikes. In many ways, it's just they like having a break from the classroom, and they like this is such a beautiful campus, it's cool to know things about it. But what students didn't like about it was all the hikes were over at Porter College, so they had to go across campus to them, and they also had to schedule it. It was two and a half hours and it was outside of class time. So that meant every student needed to find two and a half hours over the course of their quarter to walk over a quarter on a weekday, and that just felt like that's too much to ask of us. So we said, “okay”, we listened to that. And so now all the nature hikes are around Merrill, and they're very focused on Merrill, which is something that students wanted; and actually the environment, the ecology around Merrill, is different from Porter College. They have much more coastal prairie, where we have more of a conifer environment and so and now it happens within, within, within the section. So we don't ask anybody to change their schedule in order to do this.
  • Another thing: the Merrill Project, what you're doing right now is also a result of student demands. We had a previous project that was actually a project that many students found very meaningful, and it was about an event that took place in the late 1960s in the context of the Vietnam War, and a controversy over whether Hmong people in what's now Laos were being poisoned by US chemical weapons. And it's a really interesting and in many ways, kind of traumatic history, and students became very critical of that assignment. They pointed out that there actually were no Hmong voices and and and represented, and it was, and there wasn't a clear connection being made, actually, between the theme of Merrill College and the thing so a lot of students were experiencing it is kind of an interesting mystery story that was focused on the Hmong people, but in which Hmong people were not centered, and that didn't seem very narrow. So they were like, “Why don't we get rid of the assignment and do something better and that seems more meaningful to us, and that connects us to our own history?”. And actually, the Vietnam War is part of Merrill College's history, but in a different way. So that's where the Merrill Project kind of came about. So those are two examples where Merrill students were like, “No, we want this. We want this to be different.” So even though I am, as the provost, the chief academic officer, I try to do that job in a way that makes space for students to make the course their own.

And those are two examples of how it's changed, and I think it will change next year, depending [on what] you decide.

How do these answers compare to what Merrill used to be?

There is common ground to be found between the students of Merrill today and the students of 1971 even over the decades of change occurring since then. Stephens and Brown from the document felt that from group to group the theme of Merrill College was indeterminate, and, in order to determine what Merrill College would be, it would have to clarify its direction. Some of this sentiment remains now, according to Provost Aims, as students are skeptical of Merrill's current theme. Where this problem diversifies itself is in the different solutions proposed from then to now: whereas Stephens and Brown were focused on finding one meaning to Merrill, today's students are content with finding their own personal ways of defining what Merrill is. The Steering Committee referred to in the original Merrill Person document seems to have a similar role to the Merrill Student Government, which Aims has explicitly stated as being a large contributing factor in deciding what goes into the core course.

In discussing the core course, one of the suggestions made by Stephens and Brown was to update the subject of the course every year to align with the interests of the students of Merrill. This is something we as a group agreed on being told directly about being a goal of the core course, which shows a direct path of influence from then to now. One thing that we found interesting was the description of John Isbister's drastically different section. Something like this would be difficult to pull off now given that there is a Core Course Coordinator, but there is an aspect of metacognition in his plan that is apparent in the course today. This points to how the core course is malleable both by the students and faculty—the balance of which is decided by both parties.

It's all about the students.

From the student and faculty perspectives we collected to our own, we are confident that we have the answer to our initial question of “Who decides what Merrill stands for?” Although, in the end, it really comes down to the executive decision of Chief Academic Officer and Provost of the college, Aims McGuinness, to decide what Merrill's core course and main message will be for the upcoming years, this decision is heavily influenced by what Merrill values most: its students' voices.

Time and time again, Merrill students have shown their willingness to have change be made. Whether it be through student protests, displaying their art on the Merrill Moat or joining the Merrill Student Government, it is clear that Merrill students all have one thing in common. No matter where in the world or from which walk of life they come from, the Merrill Person will always be someone who has a voice and strives to be heard.
